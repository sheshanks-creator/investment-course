# Q&A Log — Topic 3: Time Horizon as an Edge

> Questions you asked during study, with answers. Appended each session.

---

**Q [2026-05-11]:** If I had a dollar for every CEO who said something similar, I'd be a millionaire as well. It seems like the example (Amazon) is again too obvious with a huge hindsight bias. What would a business that everyone has written off today that you can make the same argument for and why?

**A:** You're right to push back. The Amazon example is taught *because* it worked, which makes it feel inevitable in retrospect. In 2000, equally intelligent people looked at Amazon and saw a money-losing bookstore with delusions of grandeur competing against Walmart. The point of the lesson isn't "Amazon was obvious" — it's that *the market consistently misprices long-duration cash flows*, and that pattern repeats. So here's a current candidate where the argument applies and the outcome is genuinely uncertain.

**Alibaba (~2024)**

The market has written Alibaba off almost completely. From its peak, the stock is down roughly 75%. The reasons are real: a regulatory crackdown that erased Jack Ma from public life, forced restructuring of its fintech arm (Ant Group), persistent delisting fears from US markets, and geopolitical friction that makes Western institutional investors structurally unwilling to hold Chinese assets regardless of price. Most professional funds have reduced or eliminated China exposure entirely — not for investment reasons, but for career and mandate reasons. The forced selling is non-economic, exactly like the tobacco divestment case from Topic 2.

What the market is *pricing as permanent*: maximum regulatory hostility, sustained geopolitical escalation, and terminal competitive erosion.

What a long-horizon investor has to *judge independently*: Will China's 900 million digital consumers still shop online in ten years? Will Alibaba's cloud platform (Aliyun) still serve Chinese enterprises? Will the regulatory pressure — which was specifically targeted at preventing monopoly formation — translate into *destruction* of the underlying business economics, or merely a reset of the rules of the game?

The honest answer is: probably not destruction. E-commerce in China is structurally identical to what Amazon built — a flywheel of logistics, merchant relationships, consumer habit, and data — and Alibaba built it first at greater scale. The regulatory crackdown hurt Ant Group and forced divestitures, but Taobao and Tmall's unit economics didn't change. Aliyun is still growing double digits annually.

The uncertainty is genuine and the risk is real: the CCP could act in ways that genuinely destroy shareholder value, geopolitics could worsen, and a Chinese company delisted from US markets loses a substantial part of its institutional investor base permanently. This is not Amazon circa 2000, where the primary risk was competitive and capital. The political risk here is categorically different and harder to model.

But that's exactly the point. The *reason* the price is where it is isn't because the business is worthless — it's because the distribution of outcomes is wide and most participants are unwilling or unable to hold through that uncertainty. An investor who can genuinely assess that the probability-weighted expected value at current prices is positive — accounting for the real possibility of a bad outcome — and who has a five-to-ten-year horizon, is playing a completely different game than the institutional manager who marks to market quarterly and can't afford China exposure on their fund fact sheet.

**The key distinction from hindsight bias:** We are not saying Alibaba *will* compound like Amazon. We're saying the current price appears to assume a worse outcome than even a conservative estimate of what the business will generate if Chinese digital commerce persists — which seems more likely than not. The margin of safety is in the *gap between what's priced in and what's plausibly true*, not in the certainty of a particular outcome. That gap is what you're looking for. You won't know for years whether you were right.
