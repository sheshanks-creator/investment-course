# Topic 1: What is Intrinsic Value (and What It Is Not)

**Phase 1 — Core Foundations | Estimated reading time: ~10 minutes**

---

## A Quick Orientation

Before you can invest intelligently, you need to understand the single most important distinction in all of investing: the difference between *price* and *value*. Price is what the market charges you today. Value is what the business is actually worth. These two numbers are often quite different — and that gap is where all the opportunity in investing lives.

Intrinsic value is the formal name for what something is actually worth. The definition that matters most for a working investor is deceptively simple: **intrinsic value is the present value of all the cash a business will generate from now until it stops existing, discounted back to today.** That sentence deserves unpacking — and the second half of this lesson is dedicated to clarifying what intrinsic value is *not*, which is just as important.

---

## What Intrinsic Value Actually Is

A business exists to produce cash. Not accounting profits, not revenue, not growth — cash. Specifically, cash that can be taken out of the business and given to owners without impairing the company's ability to keep operating. Every time you think about intrinsic value, start with this question: *how much cash will this business generate for its owners over its entire lifetime?*

That cash flow stream — year by year, decade by decade — has a present value. A dollar received ten years from now is worth less than a dollar in your hand today, because the dollar in your hand can be invested and grow. Intrinsic value is the lump-sum equivalent, today, of all those future cash flows. If a business will reliably produce $10 million in free cash every year for the next 20 years, that stream of $200 million is worth a specific amount *today* — less than $200 million, because of the time cost of money, but a real, calculable figure.

This gives you a rational basis for comparison. If a business is worth $100 per share in discounted future cash flows, and the market is selling it for $60, you have found something interesting. If it's selling for $140, you haven't — regardless of how exciting the story sounds.

---

## What Intrinsic Value Is NOT

This is equally important. Intrinsic value is **not** any of the following:

- **The stock price.** The market price is what someone will pay you *right now*. It reflects sentiment, momentum, fear, greed, and liquidity. On any given day it may be far above or far below intrinsic value — and that discrepancy is the entire basis of value investing.

- **Book value.** Book value is an accounting artifact: the historical cost of assets minus liabilities as recorded on the balance sheet. A business can have a low book value and enormous intrinsic value (think Visa or Google — they require almost no hard assets to generate billions in cash). It can also have a high book value and near-zero intrinsic value (a steel mill full of equipment that can't compete anymore). Accounting and economics are not the same thing.

- **Reported earnings per share.** EPS is full of accounting choices, non-cash adjustments, and timing differences. A company can report growing earnings while actually consuming cash. Earnings tell you what the accountants have decided to show you — not what the business is putting in your pocket.

- **A precise number.** This is the trap most beginners fall into. Intrinsic value is inherently an estimate based on uncertain future cash flows. Anyone who gives you a figure like "$47.23 per share" is confusing the output of a model with reality. The correct way to think about it is as a *range*: "I believe this business is worth somewhere between $40 and $60 per share, under reasonable assumptions." The range is not a weakness — it is an honest acknowledgment of how the future works.

## A Real Company to Make This Concrete: Coca-Cola

In the early 1970s, most professional investors could see Coca-Cola's financial statements. They knew the revenues, the profits, the assets. But they priced the stock based on near-term earnings and macroeconomic mood. Warren Buffett, doing something different, looked at the *nature* of the business: a system of brand loyalty, distribution infrastructure, and repeat purchase behavior so deeply embedded in consumer habit that it would produce enormous and growing cash flows for decades, requiring very little capital to maintain.

He wasn't forecasting Coca-Cola's exact earnings for 2005. He was making a judgment: *this business, because of what it is and how it works, will generate reliable and growing cash for a very long time.* That judgment about the quality and durability of the cash flow stream — not a precise DCF model — was what gave him a useful estimate of intrinsic value. When the stock was available at a price that seemed clearly low relative to that estimate, he bought aggressively.

The lesson: intrinsic value analysis is fundamentally about understanding the *nature* of a business's cash generation. The model comes later, and only to discipline your thinking — not to manufacture false precision.

---

## The Most Common Mistake

Most people, when they first encounter intrinsic value, immediately reach for a spreadsheet. They build a discounted cash flow model, plug in numbers, and arrive at a "value." Then they compare that to the stock price and feel like they're doing sophisticated analysis.

The mistake is believing that model precision equals analytical accuracy. Your intrinsic value estimate is only as good as your understanding of the business. If you don't understand *why* a business generates cash, *what could threaten that*, and *how durable the competitive position is*, your model is just arithmetic built on guesses. Garbage in, garbage out — but printed to two decimal places.

The right sequence is: understand the business deeply → form a judgment about the quality and trajectory of its cash flows → *then* think about what that stream of cash is worth today. Skip the first step and the number you produce is meaningless, no matter how elegant the formula.

---

## Reflection Question

Think of a business you interact with daily — a brand you use, a store you visit, an app you rely on. Can you articulate *why* that business generates cash, and what would have to be true for it to keep doing so for the next 10 years? What would threaten that? This is the first step in estimating intrinsic value — not the spreadsheet, but the qualitative reasoning about cash flow durability.
