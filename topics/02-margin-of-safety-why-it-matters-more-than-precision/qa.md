**Q [2026-05-09]:** It seems that margin of safety is an additional concept if you have nailed a good range for intrinsic value. In the example on the page with Washington Post — the asset value was the clue to the intrinsic value of the business and the fat margin of safety was obvious. Can you explain further and give me another example where it is not so obvious but an intelligent investor can spot it by doing or judging things.

**A:** Yes, the Washington Post case is unusually clean because Buffett could simply tally up hard assets (printing plants, broadcast licenses, subscriber lists) and see an obvious gap. Most real opportunities don't hand you that clarity.

**Moody's Corporation, ~2000 (post-spinoff)**

When Dun & Bradstreet spun off Moody's in 2000, the market had almost no framework for valuing a ratings business. Moody's had very few hard assets — no factories, no inventory, minimal capex. On standard metrics it looked expensive: high P/E, negligible book value. A superficial screen would have passed right over it.

What an intelligent investor had to judge instead:

1. **The nature of the business model.** Moody's earns fees every time a bond is issued. It has no marginal cost of production — rating the 1,001st bond costs almost nothing. Once you understood this, you could see the earnings were nearly pure free cash flow. The FCF yield was far higher than the earnings-based multiples implied.

2. **The durability of the competitive position.** Two firms (Moody's and S&P) had been entrenched for decades. Regulation effectively required their ratings for institutional investors. A new entrant couldn't buy this trust. This made future cash flows highly predictable.

3. **The growth runway.** Global bond markets were expanding. Emerging market debt issuance was accelerating. Every new bond issued meant another rating fee. The investor could reasonably estimate that the FCF stream would grow significantly for a long time.

The margin of safety here wasn't "assets minus price." It was the **gap between what the market was pricing in** (modest, slow-growth business with new-spinoff uncertainty) **and what the economics actually were** (a capital-light tollbooth on global credit markets). Buffett estimated this intrinsic value — heavily weighted toward the durability and growth of FCF — and found the price obviously low relative to that estimate.

The key skill: you don't need a precise number. You need to be able to say with confidence, "even if I'm wrong about growth by a wide margin, the business is still clearly worth more than this price." That's the fat margin of safety in a non-obvious situation — it lives in your judgment about the business, not in a spreadsheet.
