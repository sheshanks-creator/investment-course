# Topic 8: Opportunity cost in capital allocation

**~10 min read**

---

## A Quick Orientation

Every tool in your analytical arsenal — intrinsic value, margin of safety, time horizon, circle of competence — leads to a final question: should I invest my capital *here*, or *somewhere else*? This topic addresses that comparison directly. Every investment decision is implicitly a comparison to alternatives, and failing to think clearly about alternatives is one of the most common and costly errors in investing.

## The Invisible Hurdle

When an investor evaluates an investment, they typically ask: "Is this attractive?" The more precise question is: "Is this more attractive than everything else available to me?"

Your hurdle rate — the minimum return you require before committing capital — should not be an arbitrary number. It should be determined by the best alternative available to you. If your portfolio contains a position returning an estimated 15% annually with high confidence, and you are considering a new position with an estimated 10% return, you are not adding an attractive investment — you are diluting your best one.

Buffett describes this discipline in terms of a punch card with 20 slots for lifetime investments. The scarcity of slots forces the right question: is this good enough to displace something I might invest in later? This framing prevents the "good enough" trap — accepting mediocre opportunities because they seem attractive in absolute terms.

## The Full Cost of Capital

The true cost of capital for any investor is the opportunity cost — what you give up by committing capital to this investment rather than to the next-best alternative.

Cash in a portfolio is not free. It has an opportunity cost equal to the best investment foregone. Holding cash is therefore a conscious investment decision: you are implicitly stating that no available investment justifies deploying capital at this moment. This is sometimes correct — Buffett held very large cash balances at Berkshire before 2008 precisely because he could not find investments that cleared his hurdle rate. The cash was not idle; it was waiting for the right deployment.

This also clarifies when to sell. You do not hold a position because it is a good company. You hold it because it is the *best current use of your capital* at the prevailing price. When something else becomes a better use of that capital, holding becomes the wrong decision — even if the original thesis remains intact.

## A Real Company to Make This Concrete: Buffett Selling PetroChina

In 2007, Buffett sold Berkshire's entire position in PetroChina, realising roughly $3.5 billion on an initial investment of $400 million. His stated reason was not that PetroChina had become a bad company. It was that PetroChina's price had risen to a level where the expected future return from holding it was no longer the highest-value use of that capital — other opportunities offered better risk-adjusted returns.

This is opportunity cost thinking in practice. The decision to sell was not about the quality of the business but about the comparative attractiveness of alternatives. The business was fine; the opportunity cost of holding it had risen to the point where exiting was rational.

## The Most Common Mistake

The most common mistake is evaluating investments in isolation. An investor looks at a company, concludes it is a good business trading at a fair price, and buys — without ever asking what else they could do with that capital. The relevant comparison is not "is this better than nothing?" but "is this better than my current best idea?"

The second mistake is anchoring the hurdle rate to an abstract benchmark. The right hurdle rate is dynamic — it rises when better opportunities are available and falls when nothing attractive exists. Investors who apply a fixed hurdle rate regardless of the opportunity set will deploy too much capital when good investments are scarce and too little when they are abundant.

REFLECTION:Look at your current portfolio or your list of investment ideas. What is your single best idea — the opportunity with the highest expected return for the risk? Now examine everything else: does each position genuinely clear that hurdle? If you could only hold one investment for the next five years, what would it be — and what does that answer tell you about your current allocation?
