# Topic 9: Why most investors underperform

**~10 min read**

---

## A Quick Orientation

The evidence is unambiguous: the vast majority of individual investors, and most professional money managers, underperform a simple index fund over long periods. This is not a mystery — the causes are well understood. Understanding *why* underperformance happens tells you precisely what behaviours and structures you must avoid to do better.

## The Behavioural Layer

The most consistent cause of underperformance is buying high and selling low — not as an explicit strategy, but as the emotional response to market prices. When markets rise, investors feel optimistic and add money; when they fall, investors feel fear and withdraw. This is the exact opposite of rational capital allocation.

DALBAR's annual studies have tracked this for decades. The average equity investor has earned 2–4% per year less than the funds they invested in, simply because of mistimed purchases and redemptions. They own the right instruments but participate at the wrong times. The instrument does not cause the underperformance — the investor's emotional response to price movement does.

A related failure is overtrading. Each transaction has costs: spreads, commissions, and taxes on gains. An investor who turns over their portfolio twice per year needs to generate meaningfully higher gross returns just to match a buy-and-hold investor's net returns. Activity creates friction, and friction compounds into significant drag over time. The appearance of doing something is not the same as creating value.

## The Structural Layer

Professional money managers face a structural trap that individual investors are largely free from: *career risk*. A fund manager who holds cash when stocks are expensive, or who concentrates heavily in unloved positions, can be right and still lose their job if the market moves against them before they are vindicated.

This forces institutional managers toward index-hugging behaviour — owning a diversified portfolio that broadly resembles the benchmark, ensuring that performance deviations remain small in either direction. The result is a fee charged for near-zero chance of meaningful outperformance. A manager who deviates too far from the benchmark takes career risk that is not rewarded by their compensation structure, even when their investment judgment is sound.

The individual investor who understands this has a genuine structural advantage: they are not subject to performance attribution, quarterly review, or client redemptions. They can hold a concentrated portfolio of businesses they understand deeply, do nothing during market panics, and wait years for a thesis to play out. These behaviours are rational and profitable — and structurally impossible for most professionals.

## A Real Company to Make This Concrete: 2008–2009 Mutual Fund Redemptions

During the financial crisis, mutual fund managers faced a structural catastrophe: as markets fell, retail investors redeemed in panic. Fund managers were legally required to meet those redemptions — which meant selling their best liquid positions, often at the worst prices of the decade.

The individual investor who sat still during this period, or better yet who added capital when prices were lowest, earned returns over the following decade that most institutional managers could not match — not because the individual was smarter, but because they were not subject to the structural forces that forced professional selling precisely when buying was optimal.

## The Most Common Mistake

The most dangerous belief an investor can hold is: "I will know when to get out before the next crash." This belief rationalises overtrading, market timing, and speculation masquerading as investing. The historical evidence is consistent: investors who attempt to time the market underperform those who hold through volatility, because they are almost always wrong on both the exit and the re-entry.

The second mistake is measuring performance over too short a period. Three years of underperformance does not indicate a failed strategy if the strategy is sound and the time horizon is ten years. Conversely, three years of outperformance does not validate a strategy that involved excessive risk-taking. Evaluation must match the time horizon of the strategy.

REFLECTION:Think honestly about your own investment behaviour: the market drops 35% over six months. What do you actually do? What does your emotional response tell you about the gap between your stated strategy and your likely real behaviour? What specific rule or structure could you put in place — before the next crash — to prevent yourself from acting on that emotion?
