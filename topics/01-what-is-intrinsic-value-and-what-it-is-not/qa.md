# Q&A Log — Topic 1: What is Intrinsic Value

**Q [2026-05-09]:** The word cash is used a lot here. Does cash here refer to Total Receipts (say from Invoices paid by customers) MINUS all outflows for a business? How should I interpret this - give me an example that relates it better to the concept being learned.

**A:** Close, but not quite. The cash that matters for intrinsic value is specifically **free cash flow (FCF)** — not simply receipts minus all payments.

Here's the distinction:

- **Operating cash flow** = cash collected from customers minus cash paid to suppliers, employees, taxes, and other operating costs. This is close to what you described.
- **Free cash flow** = Operating cash flow **minus capital expenditures** (money spent maintaining or growing the physical/operational capacity of the business — equipment, factories, software infrastructure, etc.).

The "minus capex" part is critical. A business might collect ₹100 crore from customers, pay ₹60 crore in costs, leaving ₹40 crore in operating cash — but if it must spend ₹35 crore every year just to keep its machines running and not fall behind competitors, the *true* cash available to owners is only ₹5 crore.

**Concrete example tied to intrinsic value:**

Take a toll road vs. a steel mill, both reporting ₹100 crore in annual profit.

- The **toll road** collects cash from vehicles, has low operating costs, and the road itself requires minimal reinvestment to keep functioning. FCF ≈ ₹90 crore. Owners actually receive nearly all the profit in cash.
- The **steel mill** also reports ₹100 crore profit, but must constantly replace furnaces, upgrade equipment, and maintain facilities just to stay competitive. Capex eats ₹80 crore/year. FCF ≈ ₹20 crore.

Same reported earnings. Wildly different intrinsic value — because the stream of *cash you can actually take out* is 4.5× larger for the toll road. This is exactly why Buffett gravitates toward capital-light businesses: their reported earnings and their real cash are nearly the same number.
