# Q&A Log — Topic 4: Circle of Competence

> Questions you asked during study, with answers. Appended each session.

---

**Q [2026-05-27]:** Is the takeaway here that — if you don't know enough at this point, you need to be humble enough to recognise that it's outside your circle of competence? Does it mean with enough time and energy spent learning you can make it part of your circle?

**A:** Mostly yes — but there's a sharper way to hold this idea than just "be humble."

The point isn't humility as a virtue. It's **honest self-assessment without ego**, because the cost of pretending to understand something you don't is catastrophic in investing. Humility is the *behaviour* the framework asks for, but the *reason* is that overestimating your understanding is what causes the largest, most permanent losses — far larger than any opportunity cost from skipping things you can't evaluate. Buffett's exact framing matters here: *"The size of the circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries is vital."* A small, sharply defined circle is more valuable than a large, fuzzy one. Most investors fail not because their circle is small but because they don't know where its edges are.

On expansion — yes, the circle can grow, but only through specific kinds of work, not just calendar time. Reading a company's filings for 10 years doesn't necessarily add anything if you read them passively. The circle expands through: reading primary sources (10-Ks / annual reports across multiple cycles, not summaries), studying multiple companies in the same industry until you understand the common economics, talking to operators and customers in the industry, and — crucially — watching the industry through at least one full downturn so you understand how the business behaves under stress.

There's also an important *speed limit*. Buffett spent 40 years explicitly avoiding technology because he judged that he couldn't reliably distinguish durable tech advantages from temporary ones. He didn't try to force himself into that circle just because tech was where the returns were. The willingness to *stay* outside your circle when it's tempting to enter is harder, and more valuable, than expanding it.

**Q [2026-05-27]:** Give me an example of Warren Buffett or Munger where they clearly stated it was within their circle of competence and why.

**A:** The cleanest example is Buffett's purchase of **Coca-Cola in 1988**.

He had drunk Cherry Coke himself for years and was famously consuming five cans a day. But that's only the surface anecdote. When he explained the purchase, he was explicit about what made it sit inside his circle:

1. **Product simplicity** — sugar, water, flavouring. He could understand the cost structure and the unit economics without specialist knowledge. There was no scientific or technological complexity that could be disrupted in ways he couldn't predict.

2. **Consumption durability** — people had been drinking Coca-Cola for a hundred years. Habits this old, at this scale, are unusually predictable. He noted that he didn't need to know whether people would drink Coke in 2030 to be confident they would in 2020 and 2025, and the compounding of even a decade of cash flow at modern multiples justified the purchase.

3. **Pricing power** — Coca-Cola could raise prices ahead of inflation in most markets without losing volume. That was visible in historical data — he could see it, not assume it.

4. **Global distribution moat** — replicating Coca-Cola's bottler network worldwide would cost any new entrant tens of billions and decades. He could quantify this.

5. **Absence of technological obsolescence risk** — and this is the key contrast he often drew with technology investing. Sugar water isn't going to be made obsolete by a better algorithm. That meant his analytical edge — patient assessment of consumer behaviour and pricing power — was the right tool for the job.

The flip side is just as instructive. For 30 years, Buffett refused to buy Microsoft despite being personal friends with Bill Gates and having Gates explain the business to him directly. His stated reason was that he could not reliably predict which technology companies would still have their competitive position 20 years out, even when the current position was dominant. Friendship and access didn't expand his circle — only the actual analytical confidence did.

The Apple purchase in 2016 is the elegant epilogue. He bought it not as a technology company but as a **consumer products company with extreme brand loyalty, ecosystem lock-in, and pricing power** — a reframing he made explicit. The reframing was honest: it was the consumer-staples lens, applied to a company that happened to make phones. That's the only way his circle expanded — not by deciding to "get into tech," but by recognising that one specific company had become understandable through the lens he already had.
